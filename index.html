COVID-19’s impact on women’s employment

Women around the world have been deeply affected by the COVID-19 pandemic, which has heightened the large and small inequalities—both at work and at home—that women face daily. However, before COVID-19, women had slowly been making some progress in the workplace. Although the total number is still slightly less than men's overall labor force, since 1984, women's labor participation rate has gradually increased to 47% of the total labor force in 2020.

However, Covid led to a major setback in female’s employment status.

Despite the progress that seemed to be made for females in the workforce, the outbreak of Covid canceled that progress and dealt a severe blow to their employment status. The unemployment rate spiked to 15.1%, and although it has been decreasing over time since hitting the maximum in April, it is still considerably higher than before the pandemic. In fact, looking at the bars indicating 2020 March and April, it is possible to check a sudden rise in the unemployment rate after the pandemic in April. Although the unemployment rate decreased for both men and women after the pandemic, its effect is much more detrimental for women.

Why are women more severely affected than men during this pandemic?

Women part time workers
Infogram

One reason we could think of is the structure of the economy and the composition of the job market. Women make up two-thirds of the part-time workers and 62% of the low-wage workers. These jobs are the servers at restaurants, the housekeeping staff at hotels or shops, the cashiers, and workers in small businesses. These people are going through reduced hours of working and thus wage, or being laid off more easily as businesses are shutting down as the pandemic persists. Moreover, almost 75% of people working for part time is involuntary, meaning that they are working for economic reason and had no choice other than working as a part time, which suggests the increase in economic insecurity these female part time workers are going to face will worsen as the situation persists.

Also, another reason is that the women tend to carry more burden of childcare than men. Even if they are working, women are expected to and actually carry out more child care. This finding is discovered across all races ranging from white to hispanic, showing that this phenomenon is not limited to one particular race, but a problem that makes many women in general to suffer. During pandemic, many school and nursery shut down or became inacessible due to lock down, which made many women unable to carry out their work and childcare at the same time.
